The Gaussian Integral

Published January 15, 2023

Recently, I saw this video showing a proof of the Gaussian integral, set to DNCE's Cake By The Ocean. It's pretty slick—I recommend watching with sound on. This inspired me to create a blogpost explaining what's being shown here.

We start with the Gaussian integral:

∫−∞+∞e−x2 dx\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} e^{-x^2}\,dx∫−∞+∞​e−x2dx

It's called the Gaussian integral because it's the integral of the Gaussian function, f(x)=e−x2f(x) = e^{-x^2}f(x)=e−x2. This function is pretty important, and shows up in a ton of different domains like physics, statistics, and signal processing. You're probably familiar with its graph:

This function has an interesting property: while the indefinite integral of f(x)f(x)f(x) cannot be written using elementary functions, the definite integral over (−∞,∞)(-\infty, \infty)(−∞,∞) can be solved analytically. This is what we will be exploring today.

The video starts by equating the integral to

((∫−∞+∞e−x2 dx)2)12\left(\left(\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} e^{-x^2}\,dx\right)^2\right)^\frac12((∫−∞+∞​e−x2dx)2)21​

This allows us to rewrite the expression as an integral of two variables:

(∫−∞+∞e−x2 dx∫−∞+∞e−y2 dy)12\left(\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} e^{-x^2}\,dx \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} e^{-y^2}\,dy \right)^\frac12(∫−∞+∞​e−x2dx∫−∞+∞​e−y2dy)21​

∫−∞+∞e−x2\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}e^{-x^2}∫−∞+∞​e−x2 converges, so we can treat this as a case of an integral multiplied by a constant with respect to yyy. Thus, we have

(∫−∞+∞(∫−∞+∞e−x2 dx)e−y2 dy)12\left(\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} \left(\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty}e^{-x^2}\,dx\right) e^{-y^2}\,dy \right)^\frac12(∫−∞+∞​(∫−∞+∞​e−x2dx)e−y2dy)21​

e−y2e^{-y^2}e−y2 does not vary with respect to xxx, so we can move it into the inner integral, yielding

(∫−∞+∞∫−∞+∞e−x2e−y2 dx dy)12\left(\int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} \int_{-\infty}^{+\infty} e^{-x^2} e^{-y^2}\,dx\,dy\right)^\frac12(∫−∞+∞​∫−∞+∞​e−x2e−y2dxdy)21​

or just

(∬R2e−(x2+y2)d(x,y))12\left(\iint_{\R^2} e^{-(x^2+y^2)} d(x,y)\right)^\frac12(∬R2​e−(x2+y2)d(x,y))21​

That x2+y2x^2+y^2x2+y2 tips us off that the next step will be to convert the integral to polar coordinates. To do so, we replace xxx and yyy with ρcos⁡θ\rho\cos\thetaρcosθ and ρsin⁡θ\rho\sin\thetaρsinθ, and our bounds of integration become ρ∈[0,∞)\rho \in [0, \infty)ρ∈[0,∞) and θ∈[0,2π]\theta \in [0, 2\pi]θ∈[0,2π]

(∬R+×[0,2π]e−((ρcos⁡θ)2+(ρsin⁡θ)2)∣d(x,y)d(ρ,θ)∣d(ρ,θ))12\left(\iint_{\R^+ \times [0,2\pi]} e^{-\left((\rho\cos\theta)^2 + (\rho\sin\theta)^2 \right)} \left|\frac{d(x,y)}{d(\rho,\theta)}\right| d(\rho, \theta) \right)^\frac12(∬R+×[0,2π]​e−((ρcosθ)2+(ρsinθ)2)∣∣​d(ρ,θ)d(x,y)​∣∣​d(ρ,θ))21​

Since we've changed the variables of integration, the differential is now given by the determinant of the Jacobian matrix.

(∬R+×[0,2π]e−((ρcos⁡θ)2+(ρsin⁡θ)2)∣(∂∂ρx(ρ,θ)∂∂θx(ρ,θ)∂∂ρy(ρ,θ)∂∂θy(ρ,θ))∣d(ρ,θ))12\left(\iint_{\R^+ \times [0,2\pi]} e^{-\left((\rho\cos\theta)^2 + (\rho\sin\theta)^2 \right)} \left|\begin{pmatrix}\frac{\partial}{\partial\rho} x(\rho, \theta) & \frac{\partial}{\partial\theta} x(\rho, \theta) \\[0.7em] \frac{\partial}{\partial\rho} y(\rho, \theta) & \frac{\partial}{\partial\theta} y(\rho, \theta)\end{pmatrix}\right| d(\rho, \theta) \right)^\frac12(∬R+×[0,2π]​e−((ρcosθ)2+(ρsinθ)2)∣∣​(∂ρ∂​x(ρ,θ)∂ρ∂​y(ρ,θ)​∂θ∂​x(ρ,θ)∂θ∂​y(ρ,θ)​)∣∣​d(ρ,θ))21​

From here, we can do a number of things to simplify the expression:

  • Reduce the exponent −((ρcos⁡θ)2+(ρsin⁡θ)2)-\left((\rho\cos\theta)^2 + (\rho\sin\theta)^2 \right)−((ρcosθ)2+(ρsinθ)2) to −ρ2-\rho^2−ρ2
  • Evaluate the partial derivatives in the determinant

This leaves us with

(∫0∞∫02πe−ρ2⋅∣(cos⁡θ−ρsin⁡θsin⁡θρcos⁡θ)∣ dθ dρ)12\left(\int_0^\infty \int_0^{2\pi} e^{-\rho^2} \cdot \left|\begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta &-\rho\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta &\quad\rho\cos\theta\end{pmatrix}\right| \,d\theta \,d\rho\right)^\frac12(∫0∞​∫02π​e−ρ2⋅∣∣​(cosθsinθ​−ρsinθρcosθ​)∣∣​dθdρ)21​

allowing us to evaluate the determinant:

(∫0∞∫02πe−ρ2⋅(ρcos⁡2θ+ρsin⁡2θ) dθ dρ)12\left(\int_0^\infty \int_0^{2\pi} e^{-\rho^2} \cdot (\rho\cos^2\theta + \rho\sin^2\theta) \,d\theta \,d\rho\right)^\frac12(∫0∞​∫02π​e−ρ2⋅(ρcos2θ+ρsin2θ)dθdρ)21​

e−ρ2e^{-\rho^2}e−ρ2 does not vary with respect to θ\thetaθ, so we can move it to the outer integral, giving

(∫0∞e−ρ2∫02πρ(cos⁡2θ+sin⁡2θ) dθ dρ)12\left(\int_0^\infty e^{-\rho^2} \int_0^{2\pi} \rho\left(\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta\right) \,d\theta \,d\rho\right)^\frac12(∫0∞​e−ρ2∫02π​ρ(cos2θ+sin2θ)dθdρ)21​

or just

(∫0∞e−ρ2∫02πρ dθ dρ)12\left(\int_0^\infty e^{-\rho^2} \int_0^{2\pi} \rho\,d\theta\,d\rho\right)^\frac12(∫0∞​e−ρ2∫02π​ρdθdρ)21​

Moving ρ\rhoρ to the outer integral yields

(∫0∞ρe−ρ2∫02πdθ dρ)12\left(\int_0^\infty \rho e^{-\rho^2} \int_0^{2\pi} d\theta\,d\rho\right)^\frac12(∫0∞​ρe−ρ2∫02π​dθdρ)21​

Finally, we simplify the inner integral…

(∫0∞ρe−ρ22π dρ)12\left(\int_0^\infty \rho e^{-\rho^2} 2\pi \,d\rho\right)^\frac12(∫0∞​ρe−ρ22πdρ)21​
