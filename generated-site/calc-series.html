The Taylor Series

A sequence is basically an infinite list of numbers. It can be thought of as a function whose domain is the set of positive integers, where the inputs are positions in the series. For example, consider the sequence 2, 4, 6, 8 …; it is defined by the function a(n)=2na(n) = 2na(n)=2n, though when talking about sequences we usually use subscript notation, so we'd write it as an=2na_n = 2nan​=2n.

Sequence Convergence §

A sequence converges if lim⁡n→∞an=L\lim_{n\to\infty} a_n = Llimn→∞​an​=L, where LLL is a finite number. If no finite limit exists, the sequence diverges.

A sequence can diverge without growing towards infinity. For example, lim⁡n→∞sin⁡(n)\lim_{n\to\infty} \sin(n)limn→∞​sin(n) doesn't exist, so we say that an=sin⁡(n)a_n = \sin(n)an​=sin(n) diverges.

Infinite Series §

The sum of the first nnn elements of a sequence is known as a partial sum.

S1=a1S2=a1+a2S3=a1+a2+a3⋮Sn=a1+a2+a3+…+an \begin{align*} S_1 &= a_1 \\ S_2 &= a_1 + a_2 \\ S_3 &= a_1 + a_2 + a_3 \\ &\vdots \\ S_n &= a_1 + a_2 + a_3 + \ldots + a_n \end{align*} S1​S2​S3​Sn​​=a1​=a1​+a2​=a1​+a2​+a3​⋮=a1​+a2​+a3​+…+an​​

The nth-term test §

Theorem: If ∑an\sum a_n∑an​ converges, then lim⁡n→∞an=0\lim_{n\to\infty} a_n = 0limn→∞​an​=0. Therefore, if lim⁡n→∞an≠0\lim_{n\to\infty} a_n \neq 0limn→∞​an​=0 or the imit doesn't exist, then ∑an\sum a_n∑an​ does not converge.

However, the opposite of this theorem is not always true; just because lim⁡n→∞an=0\lim_{n\to\infty} a_n = 0limn→∞​an​=0 doesn't mean the series ∑an\sum a_n∑an​ converges. Basically, you can use the nth-term test to prove that a series diverges, but you can't use it to prove that a series converges.

Problem: Does the series ∑n2n2+1\sum \frac{n^2}{n^2 + 1}∑n2+1n2​ diverge?

Solution: To solve this, we simply evaluate the limit lim⁡n→∞n2n2+1\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{n^2}{n^2 + 1}n→∞lim​n2+1n2​

Since the degree of the top and bottom are the same, the limit is simply the leading coefficient of the numerator over the leading coefficient of the denominator, or 11\frac1111​. This is not zero, so the series diverges.

The Direct Comparison Test §

The convergence of a series can be proven by comparing it to another series which is known to converge or diverge. This is convenient for series that are not easily tested with other methods.

Theorem: Let 0≤an≤bn0 \leq a_n \leq b_n0≤an​≤bn​ for all nnn. ∑an\sum a_n∑an​ converges if and only if ∑bn\sum b_n∑bn​ converges.

Modified Series Still Converge §

You can do all sorts of things to a series, and it'll still converge:

  • You can add or remove a finite number of terms.
  • You can multiply it by a finite constant.
  • You can add it to another convergent series.
  • You can rearrange the terms.

Sometimes modifying a series (e.g. rearranging it) allows us to prove that it converges in an easier way.

Alternating Series §

Many of the tests so far only work with nonnegative series. Adapting negative series to these tests is easy, just flip the signs. However, some series have both positive and negative terms, like the alternating harmonic series:

∑n=1∞(−1)n+1n=1−12+13−14+…\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n} = 1 - \frac12 + \frac13 - \frac14 + \ldotsn=1∑∞​n(−1)n+1​=1−21​+31​−41​+…

In general, alternating series will follow the form ∑n=1∞(−1)nan\sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^n a_n∑n=1∞​(−1)nan​.

Theorem: For an alternating series ∑n=1∞(−1)nan\sum_{n=1}^\infty (-1)^n a_n∑n=1∞​(−1)nan​, if an+1<ana_{n+1} < a_nan+1​<an​ for all nnn and lim⁡n→∞an=0\lim_{n\to\infty} a_n = 0limn→∞​an​=0, the sequence converges.

Problem: Does ∑(−1)n+1n\sum \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n}∑n(−1)n+1​ converge or diverge?

Solution: We start by recognizing that an=1na_n = \frac1nan​=n1​. From there, it's easy to see that the two conditions are met:

  • 1n+1<1n\frac{1}{n+1} < \frac{1}{n}n+11​<n1​
  • lim⁡n→∞1n=0\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac1n = 0limn→∞​n1​=0

Absolute Convergence §

Some especially quirky sequences have mixed signs, but they don't follow the strictly alternating pattern of a regular alternating series. For these offenders, we can define a concept of absolute convergence.

Theorem: If ∑∣an∣\sum |a_n|∑∣an​∣ converges, ∑an\sum a_n∑an​ converges too, and it is absolutely convergent. If ∑an\sum a_n∑an​ converges but not ∑∣an∣\sum |a_n|∑∣an​∣, the series is conditionally convergent.

We just proved that the alternating harmonic series converges, but we also know that the harmonic series diverges. Thus, the alternating harmonic series is only conditionally convergent.

Power Series §

So far we have beaten the topic of determining series convergence to death and beyond, but what are they actually useful for? It turns out that we can use series to approximate many functions (such as the trigonometric functions) which cannot be expressed using elementary operations alone.

A power series is a series of the form

∑n=0∞an(x−c)k=a0+a1(x−c)+a2(x−c)2+…\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n(x - c)^k = a_0 + a_1(x-c) + a_2(x-c)^2 + \ldotsn=0∑∞​an​(x−c)k=a0​+a1​(x−c)+a2​(x−c)2+…

where ana_nan​ is the sequence of coefficients, and ccc is the center.

We can treat the power series as a function of xxx:

Every power series has a radius of convergence RRR such that when ∣x−c∣<R|x - c| < R∣x−c∣<R the series is convergent, and when ∣x−c∣>R|x - c| > R∣x−c∣>R the series is divergent. The series may or may not converge at ∣x−c∣=R|x - c| = R∣x−c∣=R.

For all x≠0x \neq 0x=0, the value of the ratio test approaches infinity, so the series is divergent. This gives us a radius of convergence of 000.

A related concept is the interval of convergence. However, this interval is not simply equal to (x−R,x+R)(x - R, x + R)(x−R,x+R); whether the endpoints are convergent needs to be tested separately.

So why are power series useful? We'll get to that in a second. Recall f(x)f(x)f(x) from just a moment ago:
